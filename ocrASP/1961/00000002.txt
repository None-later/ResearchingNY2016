What's the Sense?

It seems to us, after reading the recent report by the
Board of Regents concerning expansion and improvement
of education in New York State, that the search for big-
ger and better things is going to result in a stalemate.

To elaborate on our convictions we ask this question:
Is it logical to increase the number of Regents scholarships
to enable more high school students to go on to college
and, at the same time, establish a uniform tuition for all
public colleges in the State? It seems to us that one negates
the other.

In order to provide more opportunities for college edu-
cations to high school seniors, it is recommended by the
Board of Regents in support of the Heald report that an
increase of Regents scholarships from five per cent to ten
per cent be more available. This we feel is needed.

We cannot agree, however, with the recommendation
that a uniform tuition charge of $300 per year be forced
upon students in all public colleges in New York State
including units of the State University.

There will be many students who will be deprived of
an education if this recommendation is accepted because
this tuition in addition to the other expenses involved in
attaining a college education is beyond their means.

Therefore we can assume that the five per cent increase
of students receiving scholarships in order to go on to col—
lege will be cancelled out by at least five per cent of the
students who will be unable to afford the additional $300
per year and consequently be deprived of a college educa-
tion.

Day Off Brings Relief

We are relieved. We were worried. but now we are re-
lieved. We expected another gruelling trek from January
to Easter without a break, but now we expect it no longer.
We have been granted that break, a breather bet . exams
and that's about all we‘ll have time for: a breath.

However, we must remind ourselves not to breathe too
much during that one day off before exams. because it is
called a “reading day,“ not a breathing day. We are pleased
that after a full semester of almost uninterrupted reading.
a special day has been set aside for the express purpose of
doing absolutely nothing but reading. And we should like
to caution those few insipid souls who might try to study
instead of read—~Remember, Big Brother is . . . etc.

We are pleased, however, that just exactly what we
must read was not stipulated. We are planning on some-
thing along the lines of the State College News. or perhaps
some long-neglected novel such as, Relaxation, which, as we
understand it, is a novel about some completely unbeliev-
able place where they have mid-semester breaks.

Then too, some of us may just sit down and react the
handwriting on the wall.

In case you hadn’t noticed we have written the above
with tongue—in-cheek. We appreciate the single reading day.
It is better than nothing. But not much. Actually we would
have much preferred to have the day after exams so we
would have some chance to recuperate. We would even
settle for two days before exams so we could do some honest
pre-exam study without having to worry about preparing
our daily lessons. But we are college students. We do not
require ordinary human considerations.

In all earnestness, we are deeply concerned about the
lack of time either before or after exams. We know that
the human body and mind require some time to complete
a transition, and that such time is beneficial to a stu—
dent‘s psychological and educational welfare We know.
We read it in a textbook.
